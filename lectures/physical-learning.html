Lecture 10

Physical Learning

How mechanical systems learn by training, adaptation, and directed aging

Paper Information

Paper A: Stern, Arinze, Perez, Palmer, and Murugan (2020), Supervised learning through physical changes in a mechanical system, Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences 117, 14843-14850.

DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2000807117

Paper B: Pashine, Hexner, Liu, and Nagel (2019), Directed aging, memory, and nature’s greed, Science Advances 5, eaax4215.

DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.aax4215

Why these two papers fit together: Paper A treats a mechanical sheet almost like a physical neural network, with training examples, a supervisor, training accuracy, test accuracy, and generalization. Paper B shows a more materials-physics route to learning, where a network “learns” desired behavior because stressed regions age and evolve differently from unstressed ones.

Big question: Can matter itself learn Definition: Change its internal structure through experience so that later responses are improved or more specialized. Why it matters: This moves learning from software into material physics. without running a digital optimization algorithm, and if so, can a mechanical system acquire useful functions Definition: Desired input-output behaviors, such as classification, auxetic response, or a targeted deformation pathway. Why it matters: A material becomes more than passive matter when it can be trained toward a function. through training Definition: Repeated exposure to chosen inputs that gradually modify the system. Why it matters: Training is the mechanism that turns multistable or aging matter into an adaptive device. , generalization Definition: Correct response to previously unseen inputs drawn from the same underlying class as the training data. Why it matters: This is what makes the learning nontrivial rather than simple memorization. , and directed aging Definition: Stress-biased evolution in which a material’s properties change preferentially along heavily loaded directions. Why it matters: It offers a physically local, naturally plausible route to learning in matter. ?

History of This Topic

Early roots: Materials have long been known to remember processing history, training, or aging, but this was usually described as nuisance, degradation, or hysteresis rather than learning.
Late 20th century: Work on neural networks, adaptive systems, and optimization made learning a precise scientific language, but mostly in software or biology rather than mechanics.
2000s: Mechanical metamaterials showed that geometry and local interactions can encode unusual responses, suggesting that function can be designed into matter rather than only computed externally.
2010s: Researchers began asking whether disordered mechanical systems could be tuned, pruned, aged, or trained into performing specific tasks such as allostery, auxeticity, and targeted response.
Directed learning in matter: Paper B is important because it reframes aging as a useful greedy learning process that can build function directly from stress history.
Supervised physical learning: Paper A is important because it imports the machine-learning language of training sets and test sets into a real mechanical system and demonstrates generalization.

Physical Learning Foundations

What is physical learning?

Definition: Learning carried out by the physical system itself through internal changes during experience, rather than by a digital optimization loop alone.

Why important: It turns adaptation into a materials property, not only an algorithmic one.

Supervised physical learning

Definition: A training process where examples are labeled correct or incorrect by a supervisor, and the material changes locally in response.

Why important: Paper A shows this can work in a mechanical sheet with crease stiffness as the learnable variable.

Directed aging

Definition: Aging under load where more stressed regions evolve faster than less stressed ones.

Why important: Paper B shows that this naturally trains elastic response without external computation at every step.

Memory in matter

Definition: Dependence of material response on how it was previously loaded or processed.

Why important: Learning is impossible unless the material can retain and use some trace of past inputs.

Model complexity

Definition: Roughly, the number of available internal degrees of freedom that can be tuned during training.

Why important: Paper A uses sheet size and number of creases as the mechanical analogue of model complexity.

Overfitting

Definition: Good performance on training examples but poor response to new examples.

Why important: Physical systems can overfit too; this is a beautiful conceptual bridge to machine learning.

Greedy algorithm in matter

Definition: Local evolution that always follows the most immediately accessible stress-relaxing path.

Why important: Paper B argues that this simple local rule can still generate sophisticated material function.

Learnable parameter

Definition: A physical quantity altered by training, such as crease stiffness or bond stiffness.

Why important: Identifying what actually changes is the mechanical analogue of identifying network weights in ML.

What to Calculate First

  • Training accuracy and test accuracyDefinition: Performance on seen examples versus unseen examples. Why first: Paper A becomes much clearer once students can distinguish memorization from generalization.: This is the fastest bridge from machine-learning ideas to mechanics.
  • Stiffness distribution or varianceDefinition: How heterogeneous the local stiffnesses become during training. Why first: In Paper A, heterogeneous crease stiffness is the physical record of learning.: It shows where the learned function is stored.
  • Poisson ratio and moduli changesDefinition: Macroscopic elastic observables such as \(\nu\), bulk modulus, or shear modulus. Why first: Paper B is strongest when students see that aging can systematically move a material toward a targeted elastic response.: These are the clearest outputs of directed aging.
  • Input-output mapDefinition: The mapping from applied forces or deformations to final mechanical response. Why first: The whole lecture is about changing this map through experience.: Students should see that learning means reshaping a response landscape, not merely changing one number.

Main Teaching Points

  • Physical learning is not just a metaphor; both papers show explicit mechanisms by which matter changes itself to improve future behavior.
  • Paper A is closest to supervised machine learning, while Paper B is closer to local adaptation or unsupervised training through stress history.
  • Generalization is the key conceptual threshold: a trained material should work on new inputs, not only repeat stored examples.
  • Local physical constraints on learning rules are different from software optimization and are part of what makes the topic interesting.
  • Disorder, multistability, and plasticity are not obstacles here; they are the substrate that makes physical learning possible.

Two Routes to Physical Learning

Route 1: Supervised classification

Paper A: The sheet sees labeled examples of force patterns and updates local crease stiffness depending on whether the response was correct.

Teaching value: Students can recognize familiar machine-learning ideas in a purely mechanical setting.

Route 2: Directed aging

Paper B: The material evolves under imposed loads because more stressed regions age faster than less stressed ones.

Teaching value: Students see that useful function can emerge even without an explicit supervisor at every update.

Shared mechanism

Common idea: Local changes accumulate to reshape the global response landscape.

Teaching value: This is the deep unifying lesson across the two papers.

Shared challenge

Common limitation: The learning rule must be physically local and compatible with real material constraints.

Teaching value: This distinguishes physical learning from ordinary software training.

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Physical learning

Definition: Learning executed by changes inside the material itself.

Why important: It is the theme of the lecture and the bridge between mechanics and information processing.

Generalization

Definition: Correct response to previously unseen inputs.

Why important: It is what makes Paper A more than a memory experiment.

Directed aging

Definition: Aging whose trajectory is biased by the applied stress history.

Why important: It is the core mechanism of Paper B.

Poisson ratio

Definition: Ratio that measures transverse strain response to longitudinal compression or extension.

Why important: Paper B uses it as a direct readout of learned elastic behavior.

Attractor structure

Definition: The partition of input space into regions that flow to different stable responses.

Why important: Paper A argues that training reshapes this structure physically.

Overtraining

Definition: Excess training that collapses useful distinctions and harms performance on new examples.

Why important: It shows that physical learners can fail in familiar machine-learning ways too.
