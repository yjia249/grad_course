History of This Topic

Early rheology: Yield stress entered soft-matter science through Bingham-type materials that behave like solids below a threshold stress and flow above it, making the topic immediately important for processing pastes, slurries, and consumer materials.
Mid-20th century: Plasticity in crystals and dislocations gave a microscopic way to think about irreversible deformation, but amorphous solids remained more mysterious because they lack obvious defects.
Late 20th century: Metallic glasses, foams, emulsions, colloids, and granular matter pushed yielding into the center of nonequilibrium condensed matter. The topic became important both technologically and conceptually: how can disordered solids fail without crystalline defects?
1990s-2000s: Shear transformation ideas, soft glassy rheology, and elasto-plastic models gave a mesoscopic language for local rearrangements, avalanches, and flow curves.
2010s: Athermal quasistatic simulations, better glass preparation methods, and scaling ideas made it possible to ask whether yielding is a true nonequilibrium transition and how brittleness depends on preparation.
Current view: Yielding is now important not only for materials design and failure prediction, but also as a deep statistical-physics problem connecting glasses, jamming, disorder, avalanches, and criticality. The review and the 2018 PNAS paper sit right in the middle of this modern shift.

Yielding Foundations

What is a yield stress material?

Definition: A material that behaves solid-like at small applied stress but flows plastically once the applied stress is large enough.

Examples: Foams, emulsions, colloidal glasses, granular pastes, metallic glasses, and many dense biological materials.

Elastic response

Definition: Reversible deformation in which stress grows approximately linearly with strain for small loading.

Why important: The whole yielding problem starts from asking when this apparently simple regime breaks down.

Plastic event

Definition: A localized irreversible rearrangement that relaxes stored stress.

Why important: The review makes clear that macroscopic yielding is built from many such events.

Shear transformation

Definition: A small cluster of particles or regions that rearranges under load and acts like an elementary plastic defect.

Why important: This is one of the main microscopic pictures behind elasto-plastic theory.

Elasto-plastic model

Definition: A mesoscopic model in which elastic loading is interrupted by threshold-triggered plastic relaxations that redistribute stress.

Why important: It is the conceptual bridge between microscopic rearrangements and macroscopic rheology.

Stress overshoot

Definition: A peak in the stress-strain curve before the system settles into flow or failure.

Why important: In the PNAS paper the overshoot evolves into a true discontinuity as annealing increases.

Shear band

Definition: A narrow region where deformation localizes much more strongly than in the rest of the sample.

Why important: Sharp brittle-like yielding is often tied to sudden shear-band formation.

AQS protocol

Definition: Athermal quasistatic shear, the idealized zero-temperature and vanishing-rate deformation protocol used in many simulations.

Why important: The main paper uses it to isolate the intrinsic yielding transition without thermal or inertial complications.

What to Calculate First

  • Stress-strain curve \(\sigma(\gamma)\)Definition: Macroscopic stress plotted against applied strain. Why first: It immediately shows elastic loading, overshoot, stress drops, and whether yielding is smooth or discontinuous.: This is the backbone observable for the entire lecture.
  • Maximum stress drop or stress discontinuityDefinition: The largest stress decrease associated with yielding or failure. Why first: In the PNAS paper it acts as the simplest order parameter for distinguishing brittle and ductile regimes.: Students can see directly how a crossover turns into a sharp transition.
  • Nonaffine displacement fieldDefinition: Particle displacements after subtracting the uniform imposed deformation. Why first: It reveals whether plasticity is diffuse or localized into a shear band.: This is the cleanest microscopic picture of brittle versus ductile yielding.
  • Susceptibilities and finite-size trendsDefinition: Quantities measuring the sensitivity and fluctuations of the stress response across samples and system size. Why first: They are needed to argue that the sharp yielding regime is a genuine transition rather than only a visual overshoot.: Use these to connect rheology to criticality.

Main Teaching Points

  • Yielding is not a single universal curve shape; the same material model can fail in qualitatively different ways depending on preparation.
  • The review helps students separate three levels of description: macroscopic rheology, mesoscopic elasto-plastic models, and microscopic plastic rearrangements.
  • The 2018 PNAS paper is important because it unifies brittle and ductile yielding within one preparation-controlled framework.
  • A stress overshoot is not automatically a first-order transition; the lecture should teach students how to tell a crossover from a discontinuity.
  • Preparation history is not a detail around the edges of the problem; it is one of the main state variables for amorphous solids.

Review-to-Main-Paper Bridge

Review message

Main idea: Amorphous solids share surprisingly universal rheology despite huge microscopic differences.

Teaching value: Students first learn the field-wide vocabulary before facing a specialized argument.

Main paper message

Main idea: The key control parameter for brittle versus ductile yielding can be the initial glass stability.

Teaching value: Students see how a broad review-level framework becomes a precise scientific claim.

Theoretical bridge

Main idea: Mean-field elasto-plastic theory predicts a first-order yielding line ending at a random critical point.

Teaching value: This is where statistical physics enters most sharply.

Simulation bridge

Main idea: Swap-prepared glasses plus athermal quasistatic shear make it possible to test how preparation controls yielding.

Teaching value: Students learn why algorithmic progress changed the science of yielding.

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Yielding transition

Definition: The change from mainly elastic response to irreversible plastic flow or failure.

Why important: It is the central event studied in both attached papers.

Pre-yielding regime

Definition: The regime before global failure where elastic loading coexists with local plasticity.

Why important: It teaches students not to confuse the onset of irreversibility with the final yielding point.

Brittle yielding

Definition: Abrupt yielding with large stress drop and strong localization.

Why important: It is the high-stability regime of the main paper.

Ductile yielding

Definition: Smooth or broadened yielding with more distributed plastic flow.

Why important: It is the low-stability regime that dominates many soft glassy materials.

Random-field criticality

Definition: Critical behavior governed by quenched disorder, akin to the random-field Ising model.

Why important: It is the proposed universality class for the brittle-ductile endpoint in the PNAS paper.

Glass stability

Definition: A measure of how deeply relaxed or well annealed an amorphous configuration is.

Why important: The review argues this variable organizes much of amorphous rheology.

Discussion Prompts

  • Is yielding best thought of as a phase transition, a spinodal, a crossover, or does the answer depend on preparation?
  • Why is the same material model able to look ductile in one regime and brittle in another?
  • What does the PNAS paper gain by combining mean-field theory with atomistic simulations instead of using only one of them?
  • Which observable would you trust most in an experiment: stress overshoot, stress drop, shear-band image, or local plastic activity map?
