Lecture 7

Aging

How glasses evolve with waiting time, rejuvenate on cooling, and recover memory on reheating

Paper Information

Scalliet and Berthier (2019), Rejuvenation and Memory Effects in a Structural Glass, Physical Review Letters 122, 255502.

DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.122.255502

History of This Topic

1970s: Physical aging in polymers and glasses was systematized by Struik, making waiting-time-dependent mechanics a central experimental language.
1980s-1990s: Spin-glass theory and experiments established rejuvenation and memory under temperature cycling, linking aging to rugged landscapes and frustration.
1990s: Simulations of Lennard-Jones glasses showed clear two-time aging in structural glasses, but not the full spin-glass-like rejuvenation phenomenology.
2000s: Experiments on molecular glasses, colloidal gels, and soft disordered solids expanded the universality of aging, while also showing that not all systems rejuvenate equally strongly.
Late 2010s: Mean-field-inspired ideas about marginal stability suggested that some structural glasses might possess a more hierarchical landscape than previously assumed.
This paper: Scalliet and Berthier provide a clear numerical example where a structural glass shows aging, rejuvenation, and memory in a single temperature-cycle protocol.

What to Calculate First

  • Mean-squared displacement \(\Delta(t_w,t_w+\tau)\)Definition: Average squared particle displacement measured over a lag time \(\tau\) after waiting time \(t_w\). Why first: It is the paper's main microscopic observable for seeing aging and rejuvenation directly.: Plot it for many waiting times and look for progressively slower dynamics in older samples.
  • Dynamic susceptibility \(\chi(t_w,\omega)\)Definition: A frequency-like readout built from particle motion over a window \(\omega^{-1}\). Why first: It gives a compact representation of all three stages of the temperature cycle on one plot.: This is the cleanest summary figure for classroom discussion.
  • Displacement distribution \(P(\Delta r)\)Definition: Probability distribution of single-particle displacements during a chosen part of the protocol. Why first: It reveals that aging, rejuvenation, and memory can involve different characteristic length scales.: Use it to connect macroscopic memory to microscopic motion.
  • Waiting-time scalingDefinition: Collapse tests using ratios such as \(\tau/t_w\) or age-dependent relaxation times. Why first: It distinguishes ordinary aging from a true restart of aging after the second temperature step.: Students learn quickly whether the second step is just slower motion or genuinely new aging.

Main Teaching Points

  • Aging means material properties depend on how long the system has been out of equilibrium, not only on its current temperature.
  • Rejuvenation is stronger than ordinary slowing down; it signals the appearance of fresh slow processes at lower temperature.
  • Memory after reheating shows that lower-temperature rearrangements can occur on smaller scales without erasing the larger-scale state prepared earlier.
  • This paper is powerful because it makes a structural glass display physics that had long been most familiar in spin glasses.
  • The interpretation in terms of marginal stability and hierarchical landscapes gives students a bridge from phenomenology to theory.

Three Stages of the Temperature Cycle

Stage 1: Aging at \(T_1\)

What happens: Dynamics slows continuously with waiting time after the initial quench.

Teaching value: This gives the baseline meaning of aging in a structural glass.

Stage 2: Rejuvenation at \(T_2\)

What happens: Lowering the temperature restarts aging instead of simply freezing the previous state.

Teaching value: This is the key evidence for a richer internal landscape.

Stage 3: Memory after reheating

What happens: Returning to \(T_1\) restores the earlier aging trajectory almost as if the middle step had been removed.

Teaching value: It shows how rejuvenation and memory can coexist without contradiction.

Microscopic picture

What happens: Motion at lower temperature occurs on smaller characteristic length scales.

Teaching value: Students can visualize why the high-temperature state is preserved while low-temperature aging still occurs.

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Aging

Definition: Waiting-time-dependent relaxation in a system that has fallen out of equilibrium.

Why important: It is the central concept of the lecture and the baseline against which rejuvenation is identified.

Waiting time \(t_w\)

Definition: Time since preparation or since the latest temperature change.

Why important: It is the extra time variable that turns equilibrium observables into aging observables.

Rejuvenation

Definition: Restart of slow relaxation after a further cooling step.

Why important: It shows that lower temperatures can reveal fresh glassy structure rather than only slower motion.

Memory

Definition: Recovery of the former aging trajectory when the system is brought back to an earlier temperature.

Why important: It demonstrates that aging at different temperatures can be nested.

Marginal stability

Definition: A regime where glassy states are barely stable and contain many soft, nearby directions in configuration space.

Why important: It is the theoretical idea used in the paper to motivate a hierarchical landscape.

Hierarchical landscape

Definition: A nested organization of metastable basins and sub-basins across scales.

Why important: It offers a natural explanation for the coexistence of aging, rejuvenation, and memory.
