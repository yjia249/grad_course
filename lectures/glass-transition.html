Glass Transition

How structural relaxation unfolds near the glass transition

Paper Information

Scalliet, Guiselin, and Berthier (2022), Thirty Milliseconds in the Life of a Supercooled Liquid, Physical Review X 12, 041028.

DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevX.12.041028

Big question: How does relaxation start and spread in deeply supercooled liquids Definition: A liquid cooled below melting without crystallizing. Why it matters: It is the regime where dynamics become extremely slow and glassy behavior emerges. close to the experimental glass transition Definition: The crossover where a liquid falls out of equilibrium and behaves mechanically like a solid. Why unresolved: Huge slowing-down appears without obvious long-range structural order. regime?

History of This Topic

1920s-1930s: Basic phenomenology of viscosity growth in supercooled liquids was established and systematic measurements began.
1940s-1950s: Foundational empirical relations (for example, VFT-type descriptions) were developed to fit temperature-dependent relaxation times.
1960s-1980s: Conceptual frameworks expanded (free-volume ideas, configurational entropy perspectives, and early microscopic theories).
1990s-2000s: Dynamic heterogeneity became central through experiments and simulations; attention shifted to spatially heterogeneous relaxation.
2010s: Major algorithmic progress (including swap Monte Carlo) enabled equilibration at much deeper supercooling in simulations.
2020s: Time-resolved microscopic studies, including this paper, map how rare local events accumulate into system-scale relaxation near experimental regimes.

What to Calculate First

  • MSDDefinition: Mean-squared displacement, averaged particle displacement squared versus time. Why first: It gives the fastest readout of cage trapping and diffusion.: Distinguish ballistic, cage, and diffusive regimes.
  • Self-intermediate scattering function \(F_s(k,t)\)Definition: Time correlation of particle positions at wavevector k. Why first: Standard way to extract alpha-relaxation time.: Obtain alpha-relaxation timescale.
  • van Hove self-correlationDefinition: Full distribution of single-particle displacements. Why first: Captures rare mobile particles hidden by averages like MSD.: Check non-Gaussian tails and rare large displacements.
  • Mobility/relaxation maps: Locate where relaxation starts and how it propagates.

Main Teaching Points

  • Relaxation starts early but only in rare localized spots.
  • These spots accumulate and connect into larger relaxed regions.
  • Late dynamics is controlled by shrinking boundaries of unrelaxed domains.
  • The paper supports an emergent dynamic facilitationDefinition: Prior relaxation events increase the probability of later events nearby. Why important: It offers a mechanism for how local events lead to cooperative slow dynamics.-like picture near \(T_g\).

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Glass transition

Definition: Liquid-to-amorphous-solid crossover with massive dynamical slowing-down.

Why unresolved: Strong dynamic changes occur without clear long-range structural ordering.

Alpha relaxation

Definition: Final structural decorrelation process in the supercooled liquid.

Why important: Sets the principal timescale for comparing glassy states.

Inherent structures

Definition: Local potential-energy minima from minimized configurations.

Why important: Separates true rearrangements from thermal vibrations.

Dynamic heterogeneity

Definition: Spatially non-uniform mobility and relaxation activity.

Why important: Key signature of the glassy regime near \(T_g\).

MSD

Definition: Mean-squared displacement as a function of time.

Why first: Fast diagnostic of ballistic, cage, and diffusive regimes.

Dynamic facilitation

Definition: Relaxation events increase future nearby relaxation probability.

Why important: Mechanistic bridge from local events to cooperative dynamics.
