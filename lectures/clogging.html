Lecture 5

Clogging

How activity can switch a microchannel between open flow and stable obstruction

Teaching Strategy For This Lecture

Why start with this paper: It is not the flashiest clogging paper, but it is excellent for teaching because the geometry, control parameters, and mechanism are very easy to parse.
What students learn first: A bottleneck can clog because particles self-organize near boundaries, and one can map that transition with just a few clean observables.
Where the excitement enters: After students understand the basic logic in a controlled microchannel, the same logic can be exported to sheep, pedestrians, capillaries, and traffic jams.
Recommended class arc: Teach Caprini et al. as the anchor paper for 60-70 minutes, then spend the last 20-30 minutes on two or three more surprising bottleneck systems.

History of This Topic

Classic granular clogging: Much of the field began with grains, hoppers, arches, and intermittent discharge through small outlets.
2000s-2010s: Clogging was recognized as a broader bottleneck phenomenon appearing in colloids, suspensions, pedestrians, animals, and microfluidic systems.
Microfluidic importance: In channels and pores, clogging became both a technological problem and a design opportunity for filtration, sorting, and flow control.
Active-matter twist: Once active particles became experimentally tunable, a new question emerged: can activity itself generate or remove clogs without externally pushing particles harder?
This paper: Caprini et al. show numerically that switching activity on creates wall-accumulated layers that block a bottleneck quickly, while turning activity off dissolves the plug.
Why useful in class: It is a clean, minimal paper that lets students learn the logic of clogging phase diagrams before moving to more surprising cross-domain examples.

What to Calculate First

  • Open/clogged phase diagramDefinition: Map of flow state as a function of density and bottleneck width. Why first: It is the central result of the paper and the most compact summary of the mechanism.: This tells students where the channel behaves like a switch.
  • Average clogging timeDefinition: Mean time needed to form a stable obstruction after activity is turned on. Why first: It quantifies how efficient the active mechanism is.: Compare how rapidly active and passive systems block the channel.
  • Density profile near walls and neckDefinition: Spatial particle concentration along the channel boundaries and bottleneck. Why first: It reveals the actual physical origin of the obstruction.: Students can see that the clog grows from wall accumulation, not from a uniform densification.
  • State switching after activity on/offDefinition: Time evolution of channel occupancy and flow under toggled self-propulsion. Why first: It shows the unclogging part, which is what makes this paper more than a standard clogging study.: This is the best observable if you want to teach the system as a controllable device.

Main Teaching Points

  • Clogging is not only a consequence of attraction or external forcing; in active matter it can arise from persistent motion and wall sticking.
  • The paper’s most useful idea is reversibility: the same system can be clogged and unclogged by toggling activity.
  • This is a good “typical” paper for class because its geometry, observables, and control parameters are very clean.
  • The teaching value of the paper is not novelty alone; it is that students can understand the full causal chain from particle dynamics to macroscopic blockage.
  • The broader literature becomes much more exciting once students realize the same bottleneck logic appears in animals, crowds, capillaries, and traffic networks.

Fancier Clogging Across Systems

Humans: Yanagisawa et al. (2009), Introduction of frictional and turning function for pedestrian outflow with an obstacle, Phys. Rev. E 80, 036110, DOI. Note: A surprisingly elegant result that a well-placed obstacle can improve evacuation by reducing conflicts at the exit. Best choice if you want a human-systems “wow” moment.
Animals: Garcimartín et al. (2015), Flow and clogging of a sheep herd passing through a bottleneck, Phys. Rev. E 91, 022808, DOI. Note: A great paper because it takes granular-clogging ideas directly into animal flow and keeps the statistics very clean. Best choice if you want the most memorable classroom example.
Animals / natural optimization: Dussutour et al. (2004), Optimal traffic organization in ants under crowded conditions, Nature 428, 70-73, DOI. Note: Not a clogging paper in the narrow sense, but a beautiful example of a living system that reorganizes itself to avoid bottleneck failure. Best choice if you want an “avoidance of clogging” angle.
Cells / capillaries: Zhong et al. (2013), Trapping red blood cells in living animals using optical tweezers, Nature Communications 4, 1768, DOI. Note: Very memorable because it literally creates and clears a clogged capillary in vivo. Best choice if you want a biophysics-heavy example.
Traffic networks: Duan et al. (2023), Spatiotemporal dynamics of traffic bottlenecks yields an early signal of heavy congestions, Nature Communications 14, 8002, DOI. Note: This is a network-scale version of clogging logic, where bottlenecks seed larger jam trees and can be forecast early. Best choice if you want to broaden the lecture beyond particulate systems.
Best canonical twist: Zuriguel et al. (2011), Silo Clogging Reduction by the Presence of an Obstacle, Phys. Rev. Lett. 107, 278001, DOI. Note: If you want one “fancier” clogging paper that is still fully canonical, this is probably the cleanest choice. Best swap if you want a sharper main paper without changing domains.

If You Want A Flashier Main Paper Instead

Best overall swap: Zuriguel et al. (2011), Silo Clogging Reduction by the Presence of an Obstacle. Reason: compact, counterintuitive, and still very close to the canonical physics.
Best living-system swap: Garcimartín et al. (2015), Flow and clogging of a sheep herd passing through a bottleneck. Reason: same statistical logic as granular clogging, but students remember it immediately.
Best human-system swap: Yanagisawa et al. (2009), Introduction of frictional and turning function for pedestrian outflow with an obstacle. Reason: turns clogging into a crowd-safety problem with a very clear design implication.
Best biophysics swap: Zhong et al. (2013), Trapping red blood cells in living animals using optical tweezers. Reason: makes clogging visceral, local, and biological.

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Clogging

Definition: Self-organized arrest of flow through a constriction.

Why important: It is the central phenomenon connecting all recommended papers.

Active Brownian particle

Definition: Self-propelled particle with persistent orientation and rotational noise.

Why important: It is the minimal active model used in the main paper.

Wall accumulation

Definition: Persistent residence of active particles near confining boundaries.

Why important: It drives plug formation in the microchannel geometry.

Burst / avalanche

Definition: Sequence of particles passing between two clogs.

Why important: Standard statistic used across granular, animal, and crowd bottlenecks.

Intermittent flow

Definition: Alternation between moving and blocked states.

Why important: It marks the regime between free flow and permanent clogging.

Switchable plug

Definition: An obstruction that can be created and removed on demand.

Why important: This is the device-level interpretation of the main PRR paper.
