History of This Topic

19th century: Phase transitions first became important through steam engines, boiling, condensation, and thermodynamics. The topic mattered because it linked heat, work, and matter in a predictive way.
Early 20th century: Magnetic and liquid-gas transitions showed that very different materials can undergo mathematically similar transformations. The importance of the subject expanded from engineering to fundamental classification of matter.
1930s-1950s: Landau theory introduced symmetry, order parameters, and free-energy expansions as a unifying language. This made phase transition theory central to modern condensed-matter physics.
1960s-1970s: Critical phenomena and renormalization group ideas revealed universality, scaling, and diverging correlation lengths. The topic became even more important because it showed why microscopic details can become irrelevant near criticality.
Late 20th century-2000s: The idea of phase transition spread into nonequilibrium systems such as glasses, jamming, percolation, and active matter. The importance of the field broadened from equilibrium phases to collective organization more generally.
2010s-present: Phase-transition ideas entered biology in a serious way, helping researchers think about tissues, flocking, morphogenesis, and cancer. This paper is important because it shows that living tissues can be mapped onto a quantitative jamming phase diagram using cell-scale observables.

Phase Transition Foundations

What is a phase transition?

Core idea: A many-body system changes its macroscopic state when a control parameter such as temperature, pressure, density, field, or activity is varied.

Teaching note: In the thermodynamic limit this change becomes sharp or singular; in finite experiments and simulations it often appears as a steep crossover.

Why do people care?

Scientific value: Phase transitions reveal how collective behavior emerges from many interacting units.

Broader value: They guide material design, explain universal patterns, and now help interpret living systems such as tissues and cell collectives.

First-order transition

Definition: A transition with a discontinuous jump in the order parameter or another first derivative of free energy, often with coexistence and hysteresis.

Examples: Liquid-gas coexistence below the critical point, melting, and many nucleation-driven transformations.

Second-order transition

Definition: A continuous transition where the order parameter changes smoothly, but fluctuations, response functions, and correlation lengths can become very large.

Examples: Ferromagnetic ordering at a Curie point and many symmetry-breaking critical points.

Landau theory

Definition: A symmetry-based theory that expands the free energy in powers of an order parameter, for example f(m)=a(T-Tc)m^2+bm^4+....

Why useful: It predicts which phase is stable, whether symmetry is broken, and often whether a transition is first or second order, even before microscopic details are fully known.

Limits of Landau theory

Key caution: Landau theory is a mean-field approach, so it can miss strong fluctuations, nonequilibrium effects, and topological mechanisms.

Why relevant here: This lecture paper is not a textbook equilibrium Landau problem, so students should learn both the power and the limits of the framework.

Order parameter

Definition: A measurable quantity that differs between phases and ideally changes systematically across the transition.

Why central: In this paper, the average cell shape index plays this role and lets the authors map a tissue-jamming boundary.

Universality

Definition: Different microscopic systems can show the same large-scale behavior near a transition.

Why exciting: It is what makes a tissue paper belong naturally in a phase-transition course.

What to Calculate First

  • Mean-squared displacementDefinition: Average squared displacement of cell centers versus time. Why first: It quickly reveals ballistic motion, caging plateaus, and long-time diffusion or arrest.: This is the fastest diagnostic for whether the tissue is fluidlike or glassy.
  • Self-diffusivity \(D_{\mathrm{eff}}\)Definition: Long-time effective diffusivity extracted from the mean-squared displacement. Why first: The paper uses it as a dynamical order parameter separating moving and arrested tissue states.: It gives a practical operational definition of the glass transition line.
  • Cell shape index \(q\)Definition: A structural measure built from cell perimeter and area. Why first: This is the paper's most striking result: a geometric quantity can locate the transition boundary.: Students should see why a structural order parameter is experimentally powerful.
  • Phase diagram in \((v_0,p_0,D_r)\)Definition: The transition surface as a function of motility speed, target shape index, and persistence or rotational noise. Why first: It is where the interdisciplinary payoff becomes obvious.: This turns a biological tissue problem into a predictive statistical-physics map.

Main Teaching Points

  • Phase-transition ideas are useful far beyond equilibrium magnets and fluids; they can organize thinking about living tissues.
  • This paper studies a nonequilibrium solid-liquid-like transition, so students must learn both analogies to and differences from textbook critical phenomena.
  • An experimentally accessible structural order parameter is especially valuable because dynamics can be hard to measure cleanly in tissues.
  • The transition depends on cell motility, persistence, and shape mechanics, showing that biology can tune collective state through single-cell properties.
  • The paper is a model example of interdisciplinary physics: soft matter, statistical mechanics, active matter, development, and cancer biology all meet in one framework.

Paper-Specific Concepts

Jamming transition

Definition: A change from a flowing or rearranging state to a rigid, arrested state.

Why important here: The paper interprets tissue solidification in this language rather than through density change alone.

Glass transition

Definition: Dynamical arrest without conventional crystallization.

Why important here: The tissue exhibits caging, slow relaxation, and heterogeneous motion reminiscent of glasses.

Confluent tissue

Definition: A cell layer with no gaps between neighboring cells.

Why important here: Because density is fixed by confluence, shape and motility become the main control parameters.

Target shape index \(p_0\)

Definition: A parameter encoding the competition between adhesion and cortical tension in a preferred cell shape.

Why important here: It acts like a tuning knob for tissue mechanics and shifts the transition surface.

SPV model

Definition: The self-propelled Voronoi model, which combines confluent tissue mechanics with active cell motility.

Why important here: It is the minimal model that lets the authors study geometry, mechanics, and activity together.

EMT and unjamming

Definition: Epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition changes cell phenotype, while unjamming refers to collective fluidization of the tissue.

Why important here: The paper asks whether these biological changes may coincide with or be illuminated by a collective phase transition.

Concept Glossary (Quick Lookup)

Control parameter

Definition: The external or tunable quantity that drives the transition, such as temperature, density, field, or activity.

Why important: In this paper the key knobs are cell motility, persistence, and target shape index rather than temperature alone.

Critical point

Definition: A special point where a boundary between phases ends or where fluctuations become unusually important.

Why important: It helps students distinguish a genuine critical transition from a broader arrested or jammed regime.

Symmetry breaking

Definition: A transition in which the ordered phase has less symmetry than the disordered one.

Why important: It is one of the main reasons Landau theory is so useful, even though this tissue paper is not a simple textbook symmetry-breaking example.

Correlation length

Definition: The spatial scale over which fluctuations or rearrangements remain correlated.

Why important: Near many transitions this length grows, which is why collective behavior can emerge from local interactions.

Active matter

Definition: Matter made of units that consume energy and generate motion or force locally.

Why important: It is what makes this tissue system richer than a passive equilibrium material.

Universality

Definition: Shared large-scale transition behavior across microscopically different systems.

Why important: It motivates the transfer of statistical-physics tools into biology.

Discussion Prompts

  • Is the tissue transition in this paper best thought of as a true phase transition, a jamming transition, a glass transition, or a sharp crossover?
  • What does the paper gain by introducing a structural order parameter instead of relying only on dynamical observables?
  • Which parts of Landau-style thinking transfer naturally to this problem, and which parts clearly do not?
  • Why is it scientifically valuable when a biological system can be placed on a phase diagram rather than described only qualitatively?
